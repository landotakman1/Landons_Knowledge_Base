# Progression Systems

## What Progression Really Means
Progression systems are what give players a sense of growth and improvement over time. Progression systems give context by evolving short-term actions into a long-term investment loop. Good progression gives players a feeling of satisfaction and rewards players for investing their time with new skills, gear, and content.

Some common forms of progression systems include leveling up, complex skill trees, gear/weapon upgrades, and even simply advancing the main storyline. A good example of this exists in the recent, massive hit *Megabonk*. In *Megabonk*, players can unlock new attacks, skill tomes, characters, and maps by reaching progressively longer and more difficult levels/maps within the game. This system is great for encouraging players to play again and again because each time a session ends, more content is available to explore for the next session.

## Layered vs. Linear Progression
Some games rely on strictly linear progression like *Super Mario Bros.* (players finish level 1, then unlock level 2, and so on). Other games use a more layered or parallel progression system. These systems allow for player advancement in multiple directions at once. Many survival/crafting games exemplify these systems well like *Valheim* where players can focus on base-building, combat, or exploration independently while still feeling the overall progression.

### The MDA Framework

A very useful way to think about progression systems (and game design in general) is through the **MDA framework** - Mechanics, Dynamics, and Aesthetics. This short paper by Hunicke, LeBlanc, and Zubek is one of the most influential pieces in game design education.

### How Progression Interacts with the Core Loop
Every time a player goes through an iteration of the [[Core Gameplay Loop]], they should get at least a small feeling of progression. In [[Player Agency and Choice]], we will investigate just how giving a player a way to direct their own progression vastly improves engagement.

Progression systems are one of the most **Powerful** tools designers have to control pacing and improve player investment in their game.
