# Resource Management Mechanics

## Resources as the Currency of Play
Virtually every game in existence gives players a currency in some form to manage. This currency can take many forms such as points, ammo, gold, health, or even time. How resources are managed in a game often defines the game's genre and difficulty.

## Common Types of Game Resources
- **Consumable** - Health packs, ammo, nitrous (resources that are used up and must be replenished)
- **Crafting or building materials** - Wood, stone, metal (resources used in crafting or building)
- **Currencies** - Gold, gems, research points, level up points (resources spent to gain items, skills, abilities, etc)
- **Information** - Maps, lore, enemy weaknesses (resources gathered through scanning or exploration)
- **Opportunity / time** - Limited daylight, limited inventory, cooldowns (a resource that dictates when and if players can engage in certain activities)

In *Stardew Valley*, gold, inventory space, and daylight left are critical resources that force the player to make difficult decisions. Poor design of resource management creates friction but elegant design creates the perfect mix of tension and skill mastery.

## Resource Relationships and Trade-offs
Resources almost never exist in isolation in today's gaming age. Resources are tied to each other and with other mechanics in the game. Stocking up on one resource often costs another (time, risk, gold). This is where [[Resource Management Mechanics]] connects directly with [[Feedback and Rewards]]. The game must clearly communicate whether the player's resource decisions are acceptable... or less than ideal.

Good resource systems make players feel that their decisions have meaning.
