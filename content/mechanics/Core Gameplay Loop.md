# Core Gameplay Loop

## Defining the Core Loop
The core gameplay loop is effectively the repeating cycle of player investigation, stimulus discovery, player action, and game response that fuels a player's desire to continue playing and investing time in a video game. It is absolutely necessary in any game that has success in it's future in one form or another. This loop is the essence that holds player attention and, in effect, creates the sensation of "fun" (if you define fun as the thing that makes you want to keep playing... debatable in many cases).

For example, in survival games like *Subnautica*, the gameplay loop is rather straightforward: player first gets dropped into an unfamiliar and dangerous environment -> player must explore to familiarize and find resources -> player must craft tools, find food, and build a base to survive -> better tools enable player to explore farther to find better resources with which to build even **Better** tools -> rinse and repeat.

## Why Strong Core Loops Matter
A finely-tuned game loop creates a rhythm and chain of events that teach the player the game mechanics in a stepwise, organic fashion. Weak game loops feel confusing or jarring. Defining and planning the core gameplay loop is often the most crucial factor in a games success or failure. The loop must feel fair yet challenging to keep players wanting to come back again and again. You can take the best AAA graphics, audio design, and voice acting money can buy and still have a failure of a game if the core loop is bad.

### Connection to Other Mechanics
Though the core gameplay loop is the foundation of a game, it rarely exists in isolation. It is almost always accompanied by [[Progression Systems]] (repeating the loop results in growth or progression) and [[Resource Management Mechanics]] (actions within the loop either consume or produce resources). Understanding the core loop is the first step in discovering what makes some games addictive while others feel like a job.
