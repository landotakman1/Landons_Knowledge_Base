Strategy and Simulation games shift the focus from direct physical control of a single character to higher-level decision making, planning, and system mastery.

## Key Distinctions
**Strategy games** emphasize competition, tactics, and optimal decision-making against opponents, other players, or challenging scenarios (real-time or turn-based). 
**Simulation games** emphasize recreating or managing a system (whether a city, a farm, a business, or a life) often with less direct win/lose pressure.

Both genres rely heavily on clear information, meaningful trade-offs, and systems that react intelligently to player decisions.

## Common Examples
- Real-Time Strategy (RTS): (*[[community/Esports & Competitive Play|Starcraft]]*, *Age of Empires*)  
- Turn-Based Strategy / 4X: (*Civilization*, *XCOM*)  
- City Builders & Management Sims: (*Cities: Skylines*, *Frostpunk*)  
- Life & Vehicle Sims: (*The Sims*, *Microsoft Flight Simulator*)

## Systems Thinking
These games are excellent demonstrations of [[mechanics/Resource Management Mechanics|resource management]] and long-term [[mechanics/Progression Systems|progression]]. A strong strategy or simulation game teaches the player how its systems work through careful [[mechanics/Feedback and Rewards|feedback]] rather than explicit tutorials alone. [[mechanics/Player Agency and Choice|Player agency]] is expressed through planning and prioritization rather than reflex.

## Design Value
Strategy and Simulation games reward patience and understanding. They show that “fun” does not always require fast action... Mastery of complex interacting systems can be deeply satisfying.
