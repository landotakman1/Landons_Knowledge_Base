Narrative design concerns how story, character, and world information are delivered through gameplay rather than only through cutscenes or text. Worldbuilding is the creation of a consistent, believable setting that supports the narrative and vibe of the game and story.

## Two Broad Approaches
- **Embedded narrative** - Story is written into the world and revealed through exploration, item descriptions, environmental details, and optional conversations
- **Emergent narrative** - Story arises from the player’s actions and the game systems reacting to those actions

Many modern games blend both. The strongest examples make the player feel that their choices and discoveries matter.

## Connection to Systems
Narrative design works best when it is supported by mechanics. A branching story feels meaningful only when it is backed by real [[mechanics/Player Agency and Choice|player agency]]. Environmental storytelling is more powerful when the player is already engaged in a satisfying [[mechanics/Core Gameplay Loop|core loop]] of exploration and discovery.

Worldbuilding also influences genre expectations. A deep lore-heavy world supports [[genres/Role-Playing Games (RPGs)|RPGs]] and certain [[genres/Horror & Survival Games|horror]] experiences, while lighter or more abstract worlds may better serve pure action or strategy games.

## Practical Reality
Not every game needs a complex story. Some of the most successful titles communicate almost everything through systems and world presentation rather than traditional narrative.
