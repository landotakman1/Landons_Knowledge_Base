A game engine is the foundational software framework that handles rendering, physics, input, audio, scripting, and many other low-level systems so developers can focus on gameplay and content.

## Major Engines in Use Today
- **Unity** - Extremely popular for indie all the way to AAA projects; strong multi-platform support and large Asset Store
- **Unreal Engine** - Industry standard for high-fidelity graphics and large teams with a powerful "Blueprints" visual scripting system
- **Godot** – Fully open-source and lightweight; growing rapidly in the [[history/The Rise of Indie Games|indie]] space with a fervent following
- **Proprietary engines** - Many large studios maintain their own game engines built tailor-made for their development process (Frostbite, Decima, etc.)

## Why Engines Matter to Design
The choice of engine influences what kinds of [[mechanics/Core Gameplay Loop|core gameplay loops]] are practical to implement, how quickly prototypes can be made, and what technical constraints the team must work within. Engines also shape the tools available for [[development/Level Design Principles|level design]] and the ease of iterating on [[mechanics/Feedback and Rewards|feedback systems]].

## Relationship to Platforms
Modern engines are designed to deploy to multiple [[platforms/index|platforms]] from a single project, but each platform still imposes its own performance, input, and certification requirements. Understanding both the engine and the target platform is essential for shipping a polished game.
