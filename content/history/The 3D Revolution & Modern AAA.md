The shift from 2D to real-time 3D in the mid-1990s fundamentally changed what games could look like and how they were designed.

## The Leap to 3D
Titles such as *Super Mario 64*, *Tomb Raider*, *Final Fantasy VII*, and *GoldenEye 007* forced designers to rethink camera control, navigation, and combat in three-dimensional space. Many classic 2D mechanics had to be reinvented or temporarily abandoned while the new language of 3D was figured out.

## Rise of the AAA Model
As production values, team sizes, and technological prowess grew, a new category of high-budget “AAA” games emerged. These projects feature:
- Large teams and multi-year development cycles
- Heavy investment in cinematic presentation and [[development/Game Engines|licensed technology]]
- Marketing budgets that often rival development costs
- Increasing reliance on live-service elements, seasonal content, and post-launch support

Modern AAA design often emphasizes spectacle, accessibility options, and long-term [[mechanics/Progression Systems|progression systems]] intended to keep players engaged for hundreds of hours.

The scale of these projects has both raised the ceiling of what is possible and made failure **extremely** expensive.
